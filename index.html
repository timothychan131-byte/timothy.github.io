The Milky Way

Our galaxy

A barred spiral galaxy home to our solar system

The Milky Way stretches roughly 100,000 light years across and is estimated to contain between 200 and 400 billion stars. Our solar system sits about 26,000 light years from the galactic center in a smaller arm called the Orion Arm.

At its core lies Sagittarius A*, a supermassive black hole with a mass roughly four million times that of our Sun. It was first directly imaged by the Event Horizon Telescope Collaboration in 2022.

The Milky Way belongs to the Local Group, a collection of more than 54 galaxies. Its nearest large neighbor, the Andromeda Galaxy, is approaching on a slow collision course and the two are predicted to merge in approximately 4.5 billion years.

Selected Facts

Worth knowing
Sunlight takes over 8 minutes to arrive

Light travels at roughly 300,000 kilometers per second, yet the 150 million kilometer distance between the Earth and the Sun means light takes around 8 minutes and 20 seconds to complete the journey.

The atoms in your body came from dying stars

Carbon, oxygen, nitrogen, and most other elements heavier than hydrogen were forged inside stars and released when those stars exploded. Every human body contains material from stellar events that occurred before the Earth was formed.

A galactic orbit takes 225 million years

The solar system travels at around 828,000 kilometers per hour, yet the distance to the galactic center is so vast that one full lap around the Milky Way takes between 225 and 250 million years. This is known as a Galactic Year.

Space produces no sound whatsoever

Sound is a pressure wave that requires matter to propagate through. The near perfect vacuum of outer space contains far too few particles to carry any wave, meaning even the largest explosion would be completely silent to a nearby observer.

There are more stars than grains of sand on Earth

Current estimates place the number of stars in the observable universe at around 200 sextillion. Every grain of sand on every beach and desert on Earth is thought to be outnumbered by roughly 10,000 stars.

Ice giants may produce diamond rain

The extreme pressure inside Uranus and Neptune is thought to compress carbon from methane into diamond crystals, which then sink toward the core. Laboratory experiments have reproduced these pressures and confirmed that diamond formation occurs under such conditions.
